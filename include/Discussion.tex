\chapter{Discussion}

UniEdgeCodec shows the most credible case for an explanation based on preserved temporal structure. On EMOB, the multi-seed results give a mean correlation of \(0.960 \pm 0.007\) and a utility retention of \(63.8 \pm 0.8\%\), which makes the claim that better preservation of temporal patterns helps anomaly detection plausible. The argument about a smoother optimization space is also consistent with the low seed-to-seed variation, although it remains an interpretation rather than a directly measured mechanism. The stronger version of the claim should therefore be phrased as a supported hypothesis, not as a proven cause.

TAEdgeCodec is the clearest case where the explanation needs to be qualified. The task-aware objective can indeed produce high utility retention, but the results are seed sensitive: on EMOB, the mean utility retention is \(67.0 \pm 13.8\%\), and the reconstruction metrics also vary more strongly than for UniEdgeCodec. This makes the idea of a harder optimization problem plausible, but the data does not support the stronger statement that the model is simply too limited for the task. A better conclusion is that the objective is more fragile and more dependent on the particular seed and dataset.

EdgeCodec is the most straightforward to explain, because it is optimized for reconstruction and the results reflect that. On smart home, it gives the best average utility retention of the compression models at \(90.1 \pm 1.9\%\), while also keeping the mean correlation and mean MSE competitive. On EMOB, the corresponding utility retention is \(65.1 \pm 10.8\%\), which shows noticeably higher seed sensitivity than UniEdgeCodec. The explanation that the model can still struggle because of competing signals in the shared codebook is plausible, but it should be presented as a likely mechanism rather than a confirmed one.

TCN-RNN remains the weakest point for the continuous baseline. Its compression ratio is much lower than that of the discrete-latent models, so the comparison is not only about reconstruction quality but also about the efficiency of the compression scheme itself. Because the baseline is only reported as single runs here, there is no seed standard deviation to compare, but the gap in compression ratio and reconstruction quality is still large enough that the explanation that continuous latent compression becomes the bottleneck at higher compression ratios is reasonable and fits the observed trade-off.

Overall, the strongest general conclusion is that reconstruction metrics and utility retention are related, but not in a universal way. On EMOB, the best reconstruction model, UniEdgeCodec, also gives the most stable utility retention, while the higher-variance models show more variable downstream results. On smart home, regression utility stays high even when reconstruction metrics differ more strongly, which suggests that the task is more tolerant to compression artifacts. That makes task structure more important than any single reconstruction metric in predicting downstream performance.

The classification task is sensitive to sparse event preservation, so lower reconstruction fidelity tends to hurt utility more directly. The smart-home regression task is more tolerant of local reconstruction errors, which is reflected in the consistently high utility retention values above 83.75\% for the discrete-latent methods.
