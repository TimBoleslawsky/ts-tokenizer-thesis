\section{Results by Method}

Below, selected empirical results are presented. The complete empirical results are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:empirical-results}. The tables summarize the reconstruction metrics and downstream utility retention values that motivate the discussion in the next chapter. First, the findings for the proposed models UniEdgeCodec and TAEdgeCodec are presented. Then the two baselines EdgeCodec and TCN-RNN are compared. 

\subsection{UniEdgeCodec and TAEdgeCodec}

\textbf{Aggregate Results} Table~\ref{tab:uni-ta-summary} summarizes the two proposed models separately for the EMOB and appliances energy datasets. UniEdgeCodec achieves the strongest and most stable EMOB correlation, while TAEdgeCodec is more variable across seeds and datasets. For the appliances energy dataset, both models preserve a large fraction of the downstream regression utility, but UniEdgeCodec does so with more stable reconstruction behavior, while TAEdgeCodec generally performs better in terms of utility retention.

When training convergence is taken into account as well, the differences in stability are further emphasized. UniEdgeCodec consistently converges over 60--70 epochs, while TAEdgeCodec can collapse and converge at epoch 5, as is the case with seed 100 and the Volvo EMOB dataset. The complete training convergence data is shown in Table~\ref{tab:epoch-convergence}, where problematic training runs are highlighted. 

Looking at the seed-specific metrics for each dataset, a relationship emerges between reconstruction quality and downstream task utility retention. However, this relationship differs substantially between datasets. For the EMOB dataset, reconstruction MSE shows a strong negative correlation with utility retention ($r = -0.85$), meaning lower reconstruction MSE is associated with higher utility. The relationship between reconstruction correlation and utility is negligible ($r = -0.002$). In contrast, for the appliances energy dataset, reconstruction MSE shows a weak negative correlation with utility ($r = -0.32$), while reconstruction correlation shows a strong negative correlation with utility retention ($r = -0.91$). When averaged across both datasets, the mean correlations are $r = -0.59$ for MSE and $r = -0.46$ for correlation with utility retention.

\textbf{Signal-Level Results} When looking at signal-specific failure modes, a pattern becomes clear. UniEdgeCodec specifically targets the weakness of the TAEdgeCodec model in reconstructing irregular and discontinuous signals. Especially in the case of discontinuous signals, this leads to major improvements in terms of reconstruction quality for the UniEdgeCodec model. Signals that are smooth and continuous are generally well reconstructed by the TAEdgeCodec model. Examples of such signals are the ``Temp 1'' signal in the Volvo EMOB dataset and the ``Press\_mm\_hg'' signal in the appliances energy dataset. Specific reconstruction plots of these two signals are further analyzed in Figure~\ref{fig:amps-signal} and Figure~\ref{fig:press-signal}.

The pattern that the reconstruction of the discontinuous signals is consistently improved by the UniEdgeCodec model holds when observing the signal-type metrics holistically. Table~\ref{tab:uni-ta-signal-specific} presents the mean MSE and correlation, averaged over the signals in each type across both the EMOB and appliances energy datasets. For both signals classified as smooth-discontinuous and irregular-discontinuous a clear improvement of both reconstruction MSE and reconstruction correlation can be seen for UniEdgeCodec. Although the UniEdgeCodec improves reconstruction quality for discontinuous signals, the reconstruction of the continuous signals usually degrades a bit. It needs to be noted, however, that the result for the smooth-continuous signal reconstruction for the UniEdgeCodec model is heavily skewed by one outlier, the ``lights'' signal in the appliances energy dataset, that performs extremely poor.

\subsection{EdgeCodec}

\textbf{Aggregate Results} Table~\ref{tab:edge-summary} shows the baseline EdgeCodec results. The model generally performs well on reconstruction tasks, especially reconstruction MSE, and is surprisingly competitive with the TAEdgeCodec and UniEdgeCodec models at utility retention without being specifically trained for it. EdgeCodec also exhibits similar instability to the TAEdgeCodec model, with standard deviations generally being larger than those observed with the UniEdgeCodec model. This is also observable in the training convergence stability, though to a lesser extent than for the TAEdgeCodec model. The EdgeCodec model never completely collapses, but can converge anywhere between epoch 23 and epoch 75 in the appliance energy case and between epoch 24 and epoch 40 in the Volvo EMOB case.

The EdgeCodec results are a useful baseline because they show that strong reconstruction does not automatically translate into the best downstream behavior. On EMOB, the model is competitive but not as stable as UniEdgeCodec. For the appliances energy dataset, the reconstruction is solid and the regression utility retention is high, which makes the model a strong reference point for the UniEdgeCodec and TAEdgeCodec implementations. The observation made for the TAEdgeCodec and UniEdgeCodec models about a use case specific correlation between reconstruction quality and utility retention can also be made for the EdgeCodec model. 

\textbf{Signal-Level Results} The EdgeCodec model also exhibits similar struggles to the TAEdgeCodec model when reconstructing discontinuous signals, but performs well on the reconstruction of smooth and continuous signals. Table~\ref{tab:edge-signal-specific} shows the mean reconstruction metrics grouped by signal type across both datasets, following the same aggregation as the UniEdgeCodec and TAEdgeCodec results. Especially the poor reconstruction correlation of the discontinuous signals here is noteworthy. The apparent ``good'' reconstruction MSE for the smooth-discontinuous signals is a measurement artifact and not indicative of proper reconstruction.

\subsection{TCN-RNN}

The TCN-RNN baseline is reported separately because it is not evaluated across the same seed set as the discrete-latent models. Its lower CR makes it a clearly weaker alternative for the compression-utility trade-off, but it still provides a clear lower baseline for reconstruction quality. In Table~\ref{tab:tcn-summary} the collected data for the TCN-RNN baseline is presented.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{TCN-RNN reconstruction and compression results. The baseline is reported from a single run per dataset; no seed standard deviation is available.}
\label{tab:tcn-summary}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{MSE} $\downarrow$ & \textbf{Corr.} $\uparrow$ & \textbf{Utility} & \textbf{Compression ratio} \\
\midrule
EMOB                      & 0.123 & 0.726 & n/a & 3.0$\times$ \\
Appliances energy         & 0.771          & 0.651          & n/a & 7.0$\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The TCN-RNN baseline is clearly behind the discrete-latent models in compression efficiency. Even where reconstruction is acceptable, the much lower CR makes it less compelling than the discrete alternatives. Increasing the CR from 3x to only 7x also already massively deteriorates the reconstruction quality.
